06 Part 1 · Simple Interest

Your First Credit Card

The Scenario

You charge $1,000 on a card with a 22% APR. You only make minimum payments. How much do you actually pay back over time?

The gap between $1,000 borrowed and what you ultimately pay is interest. Understanding how it compounds is one of the most financially important things you'll learn this semester.

Simple Interest: The Core Formula

I = P × R × T

P = Principal (starting amount)  ·  R = Annual rate  ·  T = Time in years

Ending Balance

Ending Balance = P + I

Also called Maturity Value in the textbook.

Key Conversion Rule

  • T is always in years — convert months by dividing by 12
  • 6 months = 0.5, 9 months = 0.75, 18 months = 1.5
  • R is always the annual rate — don't adjust it for monthly

Solving for Unknowns — Rearrange I = P × R × T

Three Rearrangements
P = I / (R × T) R = I / (P × T) T = I / (P × R)

Meridian Example — Solve for Rate

A client paid $19.48 interest on a $820.21 loan for 90 days (ordinary). What rate?

Manual
R = $19.48 / ($820.21 × 90/360) R = $19.48 / $205.05 = 9.5%

10 Part 2 · Compound Interest

Simple vs. Compound — Bill Smith's $80

Item Simple Interest Compound Interest
Principal $80.00 $80.00
Rate / Term 8% × 4 years 8% × 4 years
Formula I = P × R × T FV = PV × (1+i)^n
Interest earned $25.60 $28.83
Ending balance $105.60 $108.83

Why the $3.23 Difference?

With compound interest, Bill earns interest on his interest, not just the original $80. Year 2 interest is calculated on $86.40, not $80 — that snowball keeps growing every year.

The Manual Formula

FV = PV × (1 + i)^n
FV = $80 × (1.08)^4 FV = $80 × 1.36049 FV = $108.83
11 Part 2 · Future Value

The =FV() Function — Syntax First

Excel Syntax
=FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv], [type])

Argument Breakdown

  • rate — interest per period. Annual rate ÷ periods/year. Monthly at 6%: 6%/12
  • nper — total periods. Years × periods/year. 5 yr monthly: 5*12
  • pmt — recurring payment. Use 0 for lump-sum problems
  • pv — starting amount. Enter as negative (cash leaving your pocket)
  • type — optional. 0 = end of period (default). Usually omit it.
Annual Compounding · $25,000 · 6% · 5 yr
=FV(6%, 5, 0, -25000)
Monthly Compounding · same scenario
=FV(6%/12, 5*12, 0, -25000)
Manual Verify
FV = 25000 × (1.06)^5 = 25000 × 1.3382 = $33,455.64

15 Part 3 · Present Value

What Is a Future Promise Worth Today?

The Core Idea

Present value is discounting — the reverse of compounding. If money grows forward at a certain rate, then a future amount is worth less in today's dollars by exactly that same rate.

The Manual Formula

PV = FV ÷ (1 + i)^n
FV = $50,000 in 5 years i = 6%, n = 5 PV = $50,000 / (1.06)^5 = $50,000 / 1.33823 = $37,362.91
Excel Syntax
=PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv], [type])
Meridian Example
=PV(6%, 5, 0, 50000)

FV is entered as positive — it's money you receive in the future. The PV function returns a negative number because you'd have to spend (invest) that amount today. This is normal — it's the sign convention, not an error.

17 Part 3 · Present Value

FV vs. PV — Two Directions, One Logic

Future Value — Growing Forward

You have money today. How much will it be worth in the future?

Excel
=FV(rate, nper, 0, -PV)

PV is negative. FV result is positive.

Meridian Comparison Example

Client has two options: invest $20,000 today at 5% for 10 years (Option A), or receive $35,000 in 10 years (Option B). Which wins?

Option A — FV
=FV(5%,10,0,-20000) = $32,578

Present Value — Discounting Back

You're promised money in the future. What is it worth today?

Excel
=PV(rate, nper, 0, +FV)

FV is positive. PV result is negative.

Option B PV check: =PV(5%,10,0,35000) → ($21,484). Option B is worth $21,484 today vs. $20,000 cost — so Option B is slightly better. But if the rate were 6%, Option A wins. That's why the rate assumption matters.

⚠ Common Mistake

The Sign Convention Trap

❌ Wrong

Entering PV as a positive number in =FV() returns a negative result. Student sees negative, panics, and manually adds a minus sign — now the answer is doubly wrong.

=FV(6%, 5, 0, 25000) → -$33,455 ← wrong!

✓ Correct

Cash you invest today leaves your pocket → negative. Cash you receive in the future arrives → positive. Always.

=FV(6%, 5, 0, -25000) → +$33,455 ✓

Same logic for =PV(): FV is entered as positive. Result comes back negative (cost to invest today). That negative is the answer — don't flip it.

20 Key Takeaways

Three Tools, One Framework

Part 1 — Simple Interest
I = P × R × T

Time in years. Exact (÷365) vs. Ordinary (÷360). Rearrange to solve for P, R, or T.

Part 2 — Future Value
=FV(rate, nper, 0, −PV)

Compound growth. Rate ÷ periods, Years × periods. PV is negative. Rule of 72 for quick estimates.

Part 3 — Present Value
=PV(rate, nper, 0, +FV)

Discounting. FV is positive. Result is negative (cost today). The further out, the less it's worth.

The unifying idea: money has a time value. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. =FV() quantifies the growth. =PV() quantifies the discount. Both use the same rate and period logic — just in opposite directions.
