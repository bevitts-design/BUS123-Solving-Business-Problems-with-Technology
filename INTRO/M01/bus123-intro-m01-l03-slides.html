Passive User vs. Professional Operator

05
Passive User
Uses technology that others configured
  • Opens a spreadsheet someone else built
  • Types numbers into cells that already have formulas
  • Saves files wherever they land by default
  • Doesn't know why a formula works — just that it does
  • Calls IT when something breaks
  • Asks: "What does this button do?"
Professional Operator
Builds and deploys technology to solve problems
  • Designs the spreadsheet from a blank sheet
  • Writes the formulas — and understands exactly what they calculate
  • Organizes files with a deliberate system others can follow
  • Can explain any calculation to a colleague or client
  • Troubleshoots first, escalates when necessary
  • Asks: "Which tool is right for this problem?"
🎯

BUS123 moves you from the left column to the right. By the end of the semester, you will be someone who builds tools, not just someone who uses them.

The Four Case Study Companies

Where Does Tech Meet Business?

You'll work with these four companies all semester. Every calculation, every model, every analysis is grounded in one of these real business contexts.

Case Study: Tidal Goods Co.

07

Tidal Goods Co. is a retail business managing physical inventory across product lines. Retail is where margin math and inventory decisions directly determine whether the business makes money.

Technology Problems Tidal Goods Faces
  • What did we pay for this inventory — and what should we sell it for?
  • Which products have the highest and lowest profit margins?
  • How do we calculate a markdown without going below breakeven?
  • What does our cost of goods sold look like this quarter?
The Excel Answer
  • COGS tracking → Excel table with unit cost, quantity, total formulas
  • Markup calculation → Excel with cost, markup %, selling price columns
  • Markdown analysis → Excel with conditional logic and breakeven floor
  • Inventory valuation → Excel SUMIF and pivot-style summaries
🛒

Every retailer — from a local boutique to a national chain — runs on exactly these calculations. The formulas are the same. The stakes scale with the business.

Case Study: Meridian Advisory Group

08

Meridian Advisory Group is a professional services firm. At this level, technology problems are high-stakes analytical decisions — the numbers in the spreadsheet directly affect client wealth and employee livelihoods.

Technology Problems Meridian Faces
  • What is the correct gross pay for each employee this pay period?
  • What is the present value of a client's future income stream?
  • How much does this client owe in taxes on their investment gains?
  • What monthly payment does this loan structure require?
The Excel Answer
  • Payroll → Excel workbook with gross pay, deductions, overtime logic
  • TVM calculations → Excel PV, FV, PMT, NPER functions
  • Tax analysis → Excel with bracket formulas and IF logic
  • Loan modeling → Excel PMT with rate/term/principal inputs
  • Client reports → Word with embedded Excel charts and tables

Case Study: Anchor & Oak Events

09

Anchor & Oak Events is a seasonal event management business. Managing the gap between fixed costs and variable revenue is the core financial challenge — especially when income is concentrated in certain months.

Technology Problems Anchor & Oak Faces
  • How many events do we need to book to cover our fixed costs?
  • How do we manage payroll costs when staffing fluctuates seasonally?
  • What does cash flow look like in our slow months vs. peak season?
  • Where is our breakeven — and how much margin above it are we operating?
The Excel Answer
  • Breakeven analysis → Excel with fixed costs, variable costs, revenue per event
  • Labor cost modeling → Excel with headcount, hours, and rate variables
  • Cash flow projection → Excel with monthly inflows and outflows
  • Scenario modeling → Excel with input cells and formula-driven outputs
🎪

Seasonal businesses live and die by their off-season cash reserves. Knowing your breakeven number is not optional — it is the difference between surviving the slow months and not.

Case Study: Harborside Medical Center

10

Harborside Medical Center is a healthcare organization with complex payroll, diverse revenue sources, and strict compliance requirements. Here, data accuracy is a patient safety issue — not just a business preference.

Technology Problems Harborside Faces
  • What percentage of revenue comes from each payer type?
  • How do we manage payroll across staff with different role-based rates?
  • How do we identify trends in patient volume and department costs?
  • How do we present complex data clearly to non-finance administrators?
The Excel Answer
  • Payer mix analysis → Excel with percentage calculations by source
  • Complex payroll → Excel with role-based rate logic and shift differentials
  • Data analysis → Excel tables, sorting, filtering, and conditional formatting
  • Reporting → Excel charts embedded in Word or PowerPoint for leadership
🏥

Healthcare is one of the largest employers in the U.S. The skills BUS123 builds are directly applicable to every administrative and analytical role in the sector.

How Technology Shapes Decisions

14

Technology doesn't just execute decisions — it shapes them. The data you can see and analyze changes what decisions are even possible.

Before
A Tidal Goods Co. buyer needs to decide how many units to order. Without a model, the decision is a guess based on last season's memory. One wrong bet overstocks the warehouse or leaves money on the table.
After
With a properly built Excel model, the buyer runs ten order scenarios in two minutes — varying quantity, cost, markup, and expected sell-through — and identifies the order that maximizes margin under each scenario.
Result
The analyst who can build the model gets to participate in the decision. The one who cannot has to wait for someone else's output. BUS123 puts you in the first group.
💡

This pattern repeats at Meridian (loan modeling), Anchor & Oak (breakeven scenarios), and Harborside (payer mix shifts). Same skill. Different context. Every time.

Where AI Fits In — Honestly

17

AI tools — including Microsoft Copilot built directly into Excel — are becoming part of everyday business workflows. That's real. Here's a clear-eyed take:

AI is genuinely useful for
  • Drafting a first version of something
  • Suggesting functions you didn't know existed
  • Summarizing large amounts of text quickly
  • Automating repetitive formatting tasks
AI cannot replace
  • Knowing whether the answer is correct for your specific situation
  • Catching errors in a formula it didn't write
  • Understanding the business context behind the numbers
  • Taking responsibility for a wrong result
🔑

AI tools are most valuable to people who already know how to do the work. If you can't read a formula and judge whether it's correct, you can't evaluate what AI produces. BUS123 builds that foundation.

Technology Habits That Pay Off

18
  • Name every file correctly, every time — file naming is not a preference. It is a professional standard that signals care before the content is even opened.
  • Save to the cloud, not the device — local saves are one hardware failure away from lost work. Cloud saves are versioned and accessible from anywhere.
  • Use formulas, not typed values — a typed number in a result cell is invisible to anyone who needs to verify the work.
  • Document your formulas — add a comment or notes cell explaining what a complex formula does. Future-you will thank present-you.
  • Back up before you edit — make a copy of a working file before making major changes. OneDrive's version history helps, but the habit matters more.
  • Verify before you present — always open a file on a different device before presenting. What renders fine on your laptop may not work on the conference room screen.
  • Use the desktop app — browser-based Office tools are convenient but limited. For anything you're submitting, use the full application.
  • Check before you share — review permissions on shared OneDrive files. Make sure the right people can open it — and only the right people.
