## Part 1 · The Accounting Equation and the Balance Sheet

### The Foundation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Every financial report ever produced is built on a single mathematical identity:

```
Assets  =  Liabilities  +  Owner's Equity
```

This is the **Fundamental Accounting Equation**, and it never breaks. Think of it as a perfectly balanced scale. The left side represents everything a business *owns or controls*. The right side represents every *claim* against those things — either from outside lenders (liabilities) or from the owners themselves (equity).

Because of **double-entry accounting**, every transaction touches at least two sides of the equation to keep it balanced. If Harborside Medical Center buys a new MRI machine for $400,000, one of two things happens:

- If they pay cash: Assets go down $400K (cash) and up $400K (equipment) — a wash. Equation stays balanced.
- If they borrow: Assets go up $400K (equipment) and Liabilities go up $400K (loan). Equation stays balanced.

There is no transaction in the history of any business that breaks this rule.

### The Balance Sheet

The **Balance Sheet** is the formal statement that presents the accounting equation as a snapshot at a specific point in time — usually December 31. It has three sections:

**Assets** — what the business owns:
- *Current assets*: Cash, Accounts Receivable (money owed by patients/customers), Inventory, Prepaid Expenses. These convert to cash within one year.
- *Non-current assets*: Buildings, Equipment, Vehicles. These are used over multiple years. The portion of their cost allocated to each year is called **Depreciation**.

**Liabilities** — what the business owes to external parties:
- *Current liabilities*: Accounts Payable, Salaries Payable, current loan installments — due within one year.
- *Long-term liabilities*: Bank loans, bonds — due after one year.

**Owner's Equity** — the residual value left for owners after all debts are paid:
```
Owner's Equity  =  Assets  −  Liabilities
```
Equity grows through two engines: owner contributions and **Retained Earnings** (profits kept in the business rather than paid out).

> **Harborside Example:** At year-end, Harborside had total assets of $3,027,000, total liabilities of $1,305,000, and owner's equity of $1,722,000. Verify: $1,305,000 + $1,722,000 = $3,027,000 ✓

## Part 2 · The Income Statement

### Did the Business Make Money?

While the balance sheet is a *snapshot*, the **Income Statement** is a *motion picture* — it covers a period of time (usually a full year) and tells the story of whether the business was profitable.

The basic structure for a **service business** like a hospital:

```
Net Revenue (Patient Services)
   −  Cost of Services
   =  Gross Profit
   −  Operating Expenses
   =  Net Income
```

For a **retail business**, the COGS calculation includes inventory:

```
COGS  =  Beginning Inventory  +  Net Purchases  −  Ending Inventory
```

**Net Sales** is calculated after removing returns and discounts:

```
Net Sales  =  Gross Sales  −  Sales Returns  −  Sales Discounts
```

> **Harborside Example:** Gross patient service revenue was $4,680,000. After contractual adjustments of $430,000 (insurance discounts), net revenue = $4,250,000. After subtracting cost of services ($2,285,000) and operating expenses ($1,420,000), Harborside's net income = $545,000.

## Part 3 · The Statement of Cash Flows

### "Cash is King" — Why Profitable Businesses Can Still Fail

Here is one of the most important business lessons you will ever learn: **profit and cash are not the same thing.**

The Income Statement operates on the *accrual basis*: revenue is recorded when *earned*, expenses when *incurred* — regardless of when actual cash moves. A hospital can bill $680,000 in patient services in December, record that as revenue, and show a healthy profit on paper — but if the insurance companies don't pay until February, that cash doesn't exist yet. Meanwhile, staff payroll is due Friday.

The **Statement of Cash Flows** fixes this. It tracks only real dollar movements and organizes them into three categories:

| Section | What It Covers | Harborside Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Operating Activities** | Daily business: collecting payments, paying suppliers and staff | +$310,000 |
| **Investing Activities** | Long-term asset purchases/sales: buying equipment, selling old assets | −$580,000 |
| **Financing Activities** | Loans and owner transactions: borrowing, repaying, dividends | +$220,000 |

The net change in cash reconciles the opening and closing cash balances on the Balance Sheet. For Harborside: $462,000 opening + $310,000 − $580,000 + $220,000 = **$412,000 closing cash** — which must match the cash line on the Balance Sheet. ✓

## Part 4 · Trend Analysis and Ratio Analysis

### Trend Analysis: Watching the Direction

A single year's numbers don't mean much without context. **Trend Analysis** solves this by expressing each year's amount as a percentage of a *base year*.

```
Trend %  =  Current Year Amount  ÷  Base Year Amount
```

The base year is always 100%. Every other year shows whether performance improved or declined relative to that starting point.

> **Harborside Example:** Using 2024 as the base year (100%), net revenue grew to 112% in 2026 — a 12% improvement. But net income grew from 100% to 141% — meaning Harborside is getting *more efficient*, not just bigger.

### Ratio Analysis: Measuring Performance

**Ratio Analysis** uses division formulas to produce standardized metrics that can be compared across years and against industry benchmarks. Six key ratios for this lesson:

| Ratio | Formula | Industry Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities | 2.0 : 1 |
| **Quick Ratio** | (Cash + AR) ÷ Current Liabilities | 1.0 : 1 |
| **Total Debt to Total Assets** | Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets | 50%–70% |
| **Return on Equity (ROE)** | Net Income ÷ Owner's Equity | 15%–20% |
| **Profit Margin on Net Sales** | Net Income ÷ Net Sales | 8%–12% (healthcare) |
| **Asset Turnover** | Net Sales ÷ Total Assets | Varies by industry |

> **Important:** A ratio only becomes meaningful when compared to something — prior year results, industry averages, or a competitor's numbers. A current ratio of 1.5 could be perfectly healthy in one industry and a warning sign in another.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Accounting Equation** | Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity — the fundamental identity that governs all financial reporting |
| **Balance Sheet** | A financial snapshot showing what a business owns, owes, and is worth at a specific date |
| **Accrual Basis** | Revenue recognized when earned, expenses when incurred — regardless of cash timing |
| **Accounts Receivable** | Money owed to the business by customers for services already delivered |
| **Depreciation** | Spreading the cost of a long-term asset over its useful life |
| **Income Statement** | Reports revenues, expenses, and net income over a period of time |
| **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** | The direct cost of producing what was sold (Beginning Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory) |
| **Gross Profit** | Net Sales minus Cost of Goods Sold |
| **Net Income** | The bottom-line profit after all revenues and expenses are accounted for |
| **Statement of Cash Flows** | Tracks actual cash inflows and outflows across three activity types |
| **Operating Activities** | Cash flows from the day-to-day revenue-generating activities of the business |
| **Retained Earnings** | Accumulated profits kept in the business rather than paid out to owners |
| **Trend Analysis** | Expressing each year's financial data as a percentage of a designated base year |
| **Ratio Analysis** | Using division formulas to create standardized financial metrics for comparison |
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities — measures short-term liquidity |
| **Return on Equity** | Net Income ÷ Owner's Equity — measures how effectively equity generates profit |
