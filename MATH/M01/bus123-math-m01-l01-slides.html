⚠ Common Mistake #1

Mixing Units Silently Destroys Answers

Hours ≠ minutes. Dollars ≠ cents. Annual fees ≠ quarterly fees. If you multiply a quarterly headcount by an annual rate, your result is meaningless — and Excel will not warn you.

❌  =57 * 16800   ← annual fee × quarterly count = wrong period
✓   =57 * 4200   ← quarterly fee × quarterly count = correct

Rule: label your units in column headers and check them before writing any formula.

Part of a Whole

In business, fractions almost always ask: what share of the total does this piece represent? The denominator is always the whole — make sure you've defined the whole correctly before you calculate.

1
Numerator = the piece you're measuring
2
Denominator = the total universe (the whole)
3
Excel converts fractions to decimals automatically
← Excel converts fraction to decimal
=2/6→ 0.3333...
=1/4→ 0.25
=5/9→ 0.5556

Format cell as Fraction or % as needed
Worked Example · Meridian Advisory Group

Allocating the Advisory Team

STARTING ALLOCATION · 6 ADVISORS

Tax: 2 advisors → 2/6 = 1/3

Estate: 2 advisors → 2/6 = 1/3

Investments: 2 advisors → 2/6 = 1/3

AFTER HIRING · 3 NEW TAX ADVISORS

Tax: 5 advisors out of 9 total

5/9 ≈ 0.556 = 55.6% of team capacity

In Excel: =5/9 → format as % or decimal

KEY QUESTION

What's the whole? 9 total advisors.

Students who write 5/6 (old total) get the wrong answer. Always update the denominator when the whole changes.

Worked Example · Meridian Advisory Group

AUM Fee Growth — Forward and Backward

FORWARD · Project with given rate

Prior year fees: $1,200,000

Projected growth: 7.5%

In Excel: =1200000*(1+0.075) = $1,290,000

BACKWARD · Compute actual rate

Actual fees came in: $1,255,000

In Excel: =(1255000-1200000)/1200000

= 0.0458 = 4.58% — below target

THE RULE

The base (denominator) in percent change is always the older / earlier number.

Flipping numerator and denominator gives you the wrong answer — always verify which number is the starting point.

⚠ Common Mistake #2

Percent Change ≠ Percent of Total

"Revenue grew by 20%" uses (New − Old)/Old. "That client is 20% of our book" uses Part/Whole. These are different calculations — students mix them up constantly.

PERCENT CHANGE
=(New − Old) / Old
=(230000 − 200000) / 200000
= 0.15 = 15%
PERCENT OF TOTAL
= Part / Whole
= 230000 / 1500000
= 0.153 = 15.3%

Before computing: ask yourself — am I measuring change over time, or a share of a total?

Key Takeaways

Three Things to Carry Out

01
Align units before every calculation — label column headers and check the period (annual vs quarterly, hours vs minutes) before writing any formula.
02
Three Excel percent formulas: =whole*rate · =(new-old)/old · =part/whole. Format result as Percentage — never multiply by 100.
03
Percent change and percent of total are different formulas. Ask: am I measuring change over time, or a share of a whole? Pick the right structure before calculating.
