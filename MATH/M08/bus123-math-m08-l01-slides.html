Meridian Advisory Group · Client Intake

$10,000 Today or $10,500 in One Year?

The Client's Instinct
Take the future money — $10,500 is a bigger number than $10,000.
The Advisor's Question
What can $10,000 earn right now? At 6%, it grows to $10,600 in one year — more than the offer. The instinct is wrong.
The question is never which number is bigger. The question is what the money can do in the meantime.
Why Future Money Is Worth Less

Three Forces Work Against Future Cash

Inflation
$10,500 next year buys less than $10,500 today. Purchasing power erodes over time.
Opportunity Cost
Money sitting idle isn't growing. Every dollar waiting is a dollar not earning.
Risk
Future promises can fail. The further out the payment, the greater the uncertainty.
The Interest Rate, Unpacked

The Rate We Use Has Three Parts

Total Return = Inflation Rate + Real Interest Rate + Risk Premium
Inflation
Preserves purchasing power over time
Real Rate
Compensates for waiting — deferring consumption
Risk Premium
Compensates for uncertainty of future returns

The Foundation Formula

I = P × R × T

P · Principal
The original amount invested or borrowed
R · Annual Rate
The interest rate as a decimal (6% = 0.06)
T · Time in Years
Must be in years — convert months and days first
I = P × R × T
// T in years · if 6 months → T = 0.5 · if 90 days → T = 90/365
⚠ Common Mistake
The T Trap — Using Days Instead of Years
A client takes a 90-day note at 8% on $5,000. Students write T = 90 and get:
✗ WRONG — T = 90
I = 5,000 × 0.08 × 90
I = $36,000 ← impossible
✓ CORRECT — T = 90/365
I = 5,000 × 0.08 × (90/365)
I = $98.63 ← makes sense
Always check: does your answer make sense for the size of the loan?
Moving Money Forward in Time

FV = P × (1 + R × T)

=B2*(1+C2*D2)
// B2 = Principal · C2 = Rate · D2 = Time (years)
// No built-in FV function for simple interest — build it directly
What it means
You're scaling up the principal by the total growth. The (1 + RT) term is the growth factor.

1

Discounting &
Present Value
Running the Math Backward

What is a future
amount worth today?

Future value asks: what will this grow to?

Present value asks: what do I need today to end up with that future amount?

Same formula, different direction.
Future Value Direction →
PV × (1 + R×T) = FV
↕ same equation
Present Value Direction ←
PV = FV ÷ (1 + R×T)
Present Value · Formula & Excel

PV = FV ÷ (1 + R × T)

=E2/(1+C2*D2)
// E2 = Future Value · C2 = Rate · D2 = Time (years)
// (1 + R×T) is called the discount factor — it shrinks the future value back to today
The Discount Factor
The higher the rate, the bigger (1 + RT), the smaller the PV. Higher risk → lower value today.
The Economic Meaning
PV is what you'd rationally pay today for a future promise. It's the fair price of waiting.
Meridian Advisory Group · Corporate Client

A Receivable Due in 18 Months

Given
FV = $25,000 · Discount Rate = 7% · Term = 18 months
1
T = 18 ÷ 12 = 1.5 years
2
Discount Factor = 1 + (0.07 × 1.5) = 1.105
3
PV = 25,000 ÷ 1.105 = $22,624
=25000/(1+0.07*1.5)
// Result: $22,624.43
The Gap
$25,000 − $22,624 = $2,376 — that's the cost of waiting 18 months at 7%. This is why TVM exists.

Key Takeaways

Three Things That Last

01
Time erodes value. Inflation, opportunity cost, and risk all pull future money below its face value. That's why we discount.
02
I = PRT and FV = P(1 + RT) move money forward in time. T must always be in years. Never plug in days.
03
PV = FV ÷ (1 + RT) runs the math backward. It tells you the fair price of a future promise — the foundation of every valuation model.
