Inventory Valuation · Four Methods
Specific ID
Exact

Trace the exact unit sold to its purchase price. Accurate but only practical for unique high-value items — art, custom boats, jewelry.

FIFO
First In
First Out

Oldest inventory cost flows to COGS first. Ending inventory reflects the most recent prices. Most common globally.

LIFO
Last In
First Out

Newest inventory cost flows to COGS first. Lowers taxable income during inflation. Not permitted under IFRS.

WAC
Weighted
Average

Blends all purchase costs into one average unit cost. Smooths price fluctuations — popular for commodities and grocery.

The $400 swing

FIFO reports $400 less in COGS than LIFO on 20 units — meaning $400 more in gross profit. That's not a rounding error; that's a management decision showing up on the income statement.

IFRS note

LIFO is prohibited under International Financial Reporting Standards. Any US retailer planning to operate globally or go public internationally must use FIFO or WAC.

Pricing Math · Margin vs. Markup

Same numbers.
Completely different denominators.

Tidal sells a surf bag for $80. They paid $48. Profit = $32.

MARGIN
40%
$32 / $80 revenue

Profit ÷ Revenue. Used by investors and finance teams.

MARKUP
66.7%
$32 / $48 cost

Profit ÷ Cost. Used by buyers to set prices from known costs.

Margin = (Revenue − Cost) / Revenue
         = (80 − 48) / 80 = 40%

Markup = (Revenue − Cost) / Cost
         = (80 − 48) / 48 = 66.7%

⚠ The classic mistake

A buyer who targets a "100% markup" is earning a 50% margin — not 100%. Confusing the two leads to systematic underpricing across every product line.

Watch Out For This
⚠ Common Mistake — Inventory Overstatement

An inflated ending inventory count destroys two periods of accurate reporting

If Tidal's physical count accidentally includes $5,000 of phantom inventory:

  • This period: Ending Inventory ↑ $5,000 → COGS understated → Net Income overstated by $5,000
  • Next period: Beginning Inventory now too high by $5,000 → COGS overstated → Net Income understated
  • Net effect over 2 periods: The errors cancel — but both periods are wrong, and management made decisions on bad numbers

COGS ↓
Profit ↑
Period 2 — correction cascades
COGS ↑
Profit ↓

Key Takeaways · Module 06

Three things to remember

01
Inventory method choice is a strategic decision — FIFO, LIFO, and WAC produce different COGS, different profits, different taxes, and different balance sheet asset values from the exact same physical inventory.
02
COGS errors compound across periods — an overstated ending inventory this quarter becomes an overstated beginning inventory next quarter. Get the physical count right.
03
Margin and markup are not interchangeable — margin divides by revenue, markup divides by cost. Confusing them leads to systematic underpricing across every product line.
