Trade Discounts

From list price to net price
1

What Is a Trade Discount?

A reduction from the list price offered to buyers in the supply chain — retailers, wholesalers, or preferred accounts.

Volume-based
Role-based
Relationship-based

Key terms

List Price — the published catalog price

Discount Rate — the percentage off list

Net Price — what the buyer actually pays

The Core Formula

= List_Price * (1 - Discount_Rate)

The complement is (1 − discount rate). It represents the portion of list price the buyer pays.

A 25% discount → complement of 0.75 → buyer pays 75% of list price.

Trade Discount: Wetsuits

⚠ Common Mistake

Multiplying by the Discount Rate Instead of the Complement

Wrong: $189.99 × 0.30 = $57.00 — this is the discount amount, not the net price.

Right: $189.99 × (1 − 0.30) = $189.99 × 0.70 = $132.99 — this is the net price.

Chain Discounts

Stacking multiple discounts
2

What Are Chain Discounts?

Multiple successive discounts applied one after another. Each discount is applied to the result of the previous discount — not to the original list price.

Example notation:  "20/10/5" means a 20% discount, then 10%, then 5% — applied in sequence.

Chain Discount Formula

= List_Price * (1 - d1) * (1 - d2) * (1 - d3)

Multiply the complements together. For 20/10/5:  0.80 × 0.90 × 0.95 = 0.684

This means the buyer pays 68.4% of list — not 65% (which is what you'd get by adding 20+10+5).

Tidal Goods Co.

Chain Discount: Surfboard Fins

= 64.00 * (1 - 0.20) * (1 - 0.10) * (1 - 0.05)
= 64.00 * 0.80 * 0.90 * 0.95
= $43.78

Why Adding Rates Doesn't Work

Each subsequent discount applies to an already-reduced price, not the original list price.

Adding rates: 20+10+5 = 35% → $64 × 0.65 = $41.60

Chain method: 0.80 × 0.90 × 0.95 = 0.684 → $64 × 0.684 = $43.78
