Passive User vs. Professional Operator

05
Passive User
Uses technology that others configured
  • Opens a spreadsheet someone else built
  • Types numbers into cells that already have formulas
  • Saves files wherever they land by default
  • Doesn't know why a formula works — just that it does
  • Calls IT when something breaks
  • Asks: "What does this button do?"
Professional Operator
Builds and deploys technology to solve problems
  • Designs the spreadsheet from a blank sheet
  • Writes the formulas — and understands exactly what they calculate
  • Organizes files with a deliberate system others can follow
  • Can explain any calculation to a colleague or client
  • Troubleshoots first, escalates when necessary
  • Asks: "Which tool is right for this problem?"
🎯

BUS123 moves you from the left column to the right. By the end of the semester, you will be someone who builds tools, not just someone who uses them.

Case Study: Tidal Goods Co.

08

Tidal Goods Co. is a boutique retail business. Small retailers are where technology skills matter most — there's no large IT department to absorb the work, so the owner and staff do it all.

Technology Problems Tidal Goods Faces
  • How many units do we have in inventory right now?
  • What did we pay for each item — and what should we sell it for?
  • Which products have the highest profit margin?
  • Are we on budget this quarter?
  • How do we calculate COGS when inventory costs change?
The Microsoft 365 Answer
  • Inventory tracking → Excel table with VLOOKUP or INDEX/MATCH
  • Markup pricing → Excel formulas for cost, markup %, selling price
  • COGS calculation → Excel with weighted average or FIFO logic
  • Budget tracking → Excel with running totals and variance
  • Margin analysis → Excel sorted by profitability
🛍️

Tidal Goods isn't hypothetical. Every small retailer faces these problems. The inventory and pricing skills you build here are the ones a buyer or store manager uses every week.

Case Study: Meridian Advisory Group

09

Meridian Advisory Group is a financial services firm. At this level, technology problems are high-stakes analytical decisions — the numbers in the spreadsheet directly affect client wealth and compliance.

Technology Problems Meridian Faces
  • What is the present value of a client's future income stream?
  • How much does this client owe in taxes on investment gains?
  • How do we process payroll for a team with different pay structures?
  • What is the true cost of a loan after interest and fees?
The Microsoft 365 Answer
  • TVM calculations → Excel PV, FV, PMT, NPER functions
  • Tax analysis → Excel with bracket formulas and IF logic
  • Payroll → Excel workbook with gross, deductions, net pay
  • Loan analysis → Excel amortization tables
  • Client reports → Word with embedded Excel charts and tables
💼

Financial math looks clean until you're working with compound interest and tax brackets simultaneously. Getting it wrong costs clients money. Excel makes it accurate — every time.

Case Study: Anchor & Oak Events

10

Anchor & Oak Events is an event planning company. Before signing a single contract, the owner needs to know exactly how many events it takes just to cover costs — and whether the pricing makes that possible.

Technology Problems Anchor & Oak Faces
  • How many events do we need to book just to break even?
  • What happens to our cash flow in the off-season?
  • Are we pricing labor correctly when staff hours vary by event?
  • How do we model different pricing scenarios before committing?
The Microsoft 365 Answer
  • Breakeven analysis → Excel with fixed/variable cost formulas
  • Cash flow modeling → Excel monthly projection with seasonal adjustments
  • Labor cost tracking → Excel with hours × rate calculations per event
  • Scenario modeling → Excel data tables comparing pricing strategies
🎪

Breakeven math looks simple until you account for variable labor and seasonal revenue gaps. Getting it wrong means signing a lease you can't afford. Excel makes the decision visible.

🏥

Healthcare CFOs review payer mix and payroll data every month. The analytical skills you build with Harborside data are the same ones a healthcare finance analyst uses on the job.

Common Mistake

12
⚠️
Confusing "I've Used It" With "I Know It"

Most students enter BUS123 having used Excel, Word, and PowerPoint before. Very few have used them professionally — meaning to produce output someone else relies on.

  • "I've typed things into Excel" is not the same as building a formula that calculates payroll for six employees with different pay structures.
  • "I've made a slide deck" is not the same as producing a presentation a client or executive will present to a board.
  • "I know how to save files" is not the same as maintaining an organized, consistently named file system that a team can navigate without your help.
  • The gap between casual familiarity and professional competence is exactly what this course is designed to close. Be honest about where you actually are.

How Technology Shapes Decisions

13

Technology doesn't just execute decisions — it shapes them. The data you can see and analyze changes what decisions are even possible.

Before the Spreadsheet Era
  • Pricing decisions made by gut and experience
  • Inventory managed by physical count and guesswork
  • Payroll calculated by hand — slow and error-prone
  • Cash flow projections took weeks to produce
  • Business owners ran on intuition and hope
With Modern Tools
  • Dynamic pricing updated in real time with market data
  • Inventory tracked automatically, alerts trigger at thresholds
  • Payroll processed in minutes with automatic tax withholding
  • Cash flow modeled forward 12 months in an afternoon
  • Decisions are data-driven, documented, and repeatable
📈

The business that understands its own data outcompetes the one that doesn't. Building that data-literacy starts with knowing how to build and use a spreadsheet.

Where AI Fits In — Honestly

16

AI tools — including tools like Microsoft Copilot built directly into Excel — are becoming part of everyday business workflows. That's real. Here's a clear-eyed take:

AI Is Good At
  • Suggesting formulas for a problem you can describe clearly
  • Drafting first versions of documents and slide text
  • Summarizing large datasets in natural language
  • Spotting patterns in data you give it
AI Is Not Good At
  • Knowing your business's specific context and rules
  • Catching errors in data it wasn't told to check
  • Making judgment calls about what a number means
  • Building credibility with your employer — you have to do that
⚖️

The people who will use AI most effectively are the ones who understand the underlying work well enough to verify what AI produces. That's the professional operator — and that's what BUS123 builds.

Technology Habits That Pay Off

17
  • Name files so others can find them — not just you, right now. A well-named file is a professional courtesy.
  • Save as you work — don't wait until you're done. Autosave in OneDrive helps, but it's not a replacement for the habit.
  • Never put important data in only one place — OneDrive syncs to the cloud, but know where your files actually live.
  • Document your formulas — add a comment or a notes cell explaining what a complex formula does. Future-you will thank present-you.
  • Back up before you edit — make a copy of a working file before making major changes. OneDrive's version history helps, but the habit matters more.
  • Verify before you present — always open a file on a different device or at a different time before presenting it. What renders fine on your laptop may not work on the conference room screen.
  • Use the real app — browser-based Office tools are convenient but limited. For anything you're going to submit, use the desktop app.
  • Check before you share — review permissions on shared OneDrive files. Make sure the right people can open it.
