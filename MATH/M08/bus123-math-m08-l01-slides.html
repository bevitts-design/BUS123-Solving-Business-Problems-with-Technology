Part 1 · Why Time Affects Value

$10,000 Today
or $10,500 in One Year?

The Client's Instinct

Take the future money — $10,500 is a bigger number. More is more.

The Advisor's Question

What can $10,000 earn right now? At 6%, it grows to $10,600 in one year — more than the offer.

The instinct is wrong. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow — not because of the face value, but because of what that dollar can earn between now and then. This is the Time Value of Money.

Part 1 · Why Time Affects Value

Three Forces Work Against Future Cash

Inflation

$10,500 next year buys less than $10,500 today. Prices rise. Every dollar of future value is eroded by the general increase in prices.

Opportunity Cost

Money sitting idle isn't growing. Every dollar waiting for a future payment is a dollar not earning a return somewhere else right now.

Risk

Future promises can fail. The farther out a payment, the greater the chance it never arrives. Uncertainty reduces value.

The discount rate captures all three simultaneously. A 7% rate might be 3% inflation + 2% real return + 2% risk premium. Every component has economic meaning.

Part 1 · The Discount Rate

The Rate We Use Has Three Parts

Total Discount Rate = Inflation Rate + Real Interest Rate + Risk Premium
Inflation Rate

Preserves purchasing power over time. Without it, you lose real value just by waiting.

Real Interest Rate

Compensates for waiting — deferring consumption today in exchange for more tomorrow.

Risk Premium

Compensates for uncertainty. Riskier future payments demand a higher premium.

Part 2 · Simple Interest

I = P × R × T

P
Principal

The original amount borrowed or invested. The base amount before any interest.

R
Annual Rate

Always enter as a decimal. 6% = 0.06. The rate is always stated annually.

T
Time in Years

Must be in years. 6 months = 0.5. 90 days = 90÷365.

Excel — No Built-in Function for Simple Interest
// I = P × R × T — build directly in a cell =C2*D2*E2 // C2 = Principal · D2 = Rate · E2 = Time (years) =C2*(1+D2*E2) // FV = P × (1 + R×T) — ending balance
Part 2 · Simple Interest

Day-Count Methods

Exact Interest ÷ 365

T = days ÷ 365. Used by the Federal Reserve and the US government. Every day counts as exactly 1/365 of a year.

Ordinary Interest ÷ 360 (Banker's Rule)

T = days ÷ 360. Used by most commercial banks. Produces slightly more interest — intentionally favoring the lender.

Meridian Example — $50,000 at 5% for 124 days

Exact Interest (÷365)
T = 124 ÷ 365 = 0.3397
I = 50,000 × 0.05 × 0.3397
= $849.32
Ordinary Interest (÷360)
T = 124 ÷ 360 = 0.3444
I = 50,000 × 0.05 × 0.3444
= $861.11

Difference: $11.79. Across a $500M loan portfolio, the bank method generates significantly more revenue.

Part 2 · Future Value

Moving Money Forward — FV = P × (1 + R × T)

Excel Formula — Simple Interest FV
=B2*(1+C2*D2)// B2=Principal · C2=Rate · D2=Time(yrs)// No =FV() function for simple interest// Build the growth factor directly
What the Growth Factor Means
At 5.5% for 9 months:
(1 + 0.055 × 0.75) = 1.04125
Every $1 of principal → $1.04125
$8,000 × 1.04125 = $8,330.00

Key Insight

The term (1 + R×T) is the growth factor. It tells you how many times larger your money becomes.

Simple interest grows linearly. Double the time, double the interest. Next module: compound interest, where growth is exponential.

Part 2 · Worked Example

Meridian Client — Short-Term Corporate Note

Given

P = $8,000  ·  R = 5.5% annual  ·  Term = 9 months

Step-by-Step
① T = 9 ÷ 12 = 0.75 years
② I = 8,000 × 0.055 × 0.75 = $330.00
③ FV = 8,000 + 330 = $8,330.00
Excel — Single Formula
=8000*(1+0.055*0.75)// Or better: use cell references=B3*(1+C3*D3)// B3=8000 · C3=0.055 · D3=0.75

Never hardcode. Enter each input in its own labeled cell. Use cell references in the formula. This makes the model auditable — anyone can trace every number.

Part 2 · Simple Interest

Solving for the Unknown Variable

Three Rearrangements

Solve for PrincipalP = I ÷ (R × T)
Solve for RateR = I ÷ (P × T)
Solve for TimeT = I ÷ (P × R)

Real analysts solve for unknowns constantly — a client paid $X in interest, at Y% rate, what was the original loan? Always rearrange before opening Excel.

Meridian Example — Find the Principal

A client paid $19.48 in interest at 9.5% for 90 days (ordinary interest). What was the original principal?

Solve
T = 90 ÷ 360 = 0.25 years
P = 19.48 ÷ (0.095 × 0.25)
P = 19.48 ÷ 0.02375
= $820.21
Excel
=B5/(B3*B4)// B3=rate · B4=time · B5=interest

Part 3 · Present Value

What is a future
amount worth today?

Future Value moves money forward — you know what you have today, and you want to know what it will be worth later. Present Value runs the same logic backward — you know a future promise, and you need to know its fair value right now.

Forward →

FV = P × (1 + R × T)

← Backward

PV = FV ÷ (1 + R × T)

Part 3 · Present Value

PV = FV ÷ (1 + R × T) — The Discount Factor

Excel — Manual PV (Simple Interest)
=E2/(1+C2*D2)// E2=FV · C2=Rate · D2=Time(yrs)// (1+R×T) is the discount factor
Meridian — Receivable Due in 18 Months
FV = $25,000 · R = 7% · T = 18 months
① T = 18 ÷ 12 = 1.5 years
② DF = 1 + (0.07 × 1.5) = 1.105
③ PV = 25,000 ÷ 1.105 = $22,624.43

The $2,376 Gap

$25,000 − $22,624 = $2,376

That gap is the cost of waiting — 18 months of opportunity cost and risk at 7%. This is the dollar value of time. Every discounted cash flow analysis is built on this single calculation.

Higher rate or longer time → larger discount factor → smaller PV. The formula always produces PV < FV.

Meridian Example

Always run the round trip.

Part 3 · Excel PV Function

The Sign Convention — What the Negative Means

Excel's Rule

Outflows (money leaving you): negative
Inflows (money coming to you): positive

When you invest to earn a future payment, the investment is an outflow — negative. The PV result is the amount you must put in today to receive the future FV. It is always negative for receivables.

Correct Usage
=PV(5%, 3, 0, 115) → −$99.34// FV positive → PV negative (correct)
Wrong Sign — Still Returns a Number
=PV(5%, 3, 0, −115) → +$99.34// No error — but the meaning is flipped

Sanity check every time: PV < FV in absolute terms. If your PV is larger than FV, the sign is wrong or you multiplied instead of dividing.

The farther away a payment, the less it is worth today. At 5%, a $100 promise in 10 years is worth only $60.95 now.

Part 3 · Putting It Together

FV and PV — Two Directions, One Logic

Future Value — Growing Forward

You have money today. How much will it be worth in the future?

FV = P × (1 + R × T)
$99.34 × (1 + 0.05 × 3) = $114.00

Present Value — Discounting Back

You expect money in the future. What is it worth today?

PV = FV ÷ (1 + R × T)
$114 ÷ (1 + 0.05 × 3) = $99.34

The Round Trip — Always Verify

Excel Round Trip Check
=PV(5%, 3, 0, 114) → −$99.34
=FV(5%, 3, 0, −99.34) → $114.00 ✓

FV and PV are mathematical inverses. If they don't round-trip correctly, something is wrong with your inputs. Always run this check during the activity.

Coming in M09: =FV() and =PV() with compound interest — where the exponent n replaces simple multiplication, and growth becomes exponential.

⚠ Common Mistake

The Negative PV Panic

❌ What Students Do

They see −$22,624 and assume it is an error. They manually add a minus sign to make it positive. Now the formula returns +$22,624 — which looks right but is actually a completely wrong interpretation.

The Confusion
=PV(7%, 1.5, 0, 25000) → −$22,624// Student adds minus: −(−22624) = +22624// Now they've broken the sign meaning
✓ What the Negative Actually Means

The negative sign means outflow — the amount you would need to invest today to receive $25,000 in 18 months at 7%. It is not an error. It is the answer.

Verify with Round Trip
=FV(7%, 1.5, 0, −22624) → $25,000 ✓// Negative PV + positive FV = consistent
