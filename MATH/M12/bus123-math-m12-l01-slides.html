Central Tendency · Mean

The Arithmetic Average

Add up all the values in a dataset, then divide by how many values there are. The mean is the most common "average" — but it can be pulled off-center by extreme outliers.

// Excel syntax — always first
=AVERAGE(number1, [number2], ...)

// Manual calculation
Mean = Σx ÷ n

Harborside Medical

Daily patient wait times (minutes): 12, 15, 18, 11, 42

=AVERAGE(A2:A6) → 19.6 min

(12+15+18+11+42) ÷ 5 = 98 ÷ 5

⚠ Watch Out

The 42-minute outlier pulls the mean up to 19.6. Most days were 11–18 min. The mean alone is misleading here.

Central Tendency · Median

The Middle Value

Sort the values from smallest to largest. The median is the middle number. If there's an even count, average the two middle values.

=MEDIAN(number1, [number2], ...)
// Returns the middle value of a sorted range

Harborside Wait Times — sorted

11
12
15
18
42

=MEDIAN(A2:A6) → 15 min

Why median > mean here?

Mean = 19.6 min. Median = 15 min. The median is a more honest picture of typical patient wait times because it isn't dragged upward by that one 42-minute day.

RULE OF THUMB

Use median when your data has outliers or is skewed. Use mean when the data is symmetric.

Central Tendency · Mode

The Most Frequent Value

The mode is the value that appears most often. A dataset can have no mode, one mode, or be bimodal (two values tie for most frequent).

=MODE.SNGL(number1, [number2], ...)
// Returns the most frequent value

=MODE.MULT(number1, [number2], ...)
// Returns ALL modes (array — use Ctrl+Shift+Enter)

Harborside · Diagnosis Codes Filed (10 days)

J06.9 Z00.0 J06.9 Z00.0 Z00.0 M54.5 J06.9 M54.5 Z00.0 J06.9

Mode → Z00.0 (routine checkup) · appears 4×

Why it matters: The mode tells Harborside which visit type to staff for most heavily. Routine checkups dominate — schedule accordingly.

Dispersion · Why Spread Matters

Same mean — very different data

Hospital A · Wait Times (min)

18 19 20 21 22

Mean = 20 min · SD ≈ 1.6 min

Consistent and predictable — low spread

vs

Hospital B · Wait Times (min)

2 8 20 35 35

Mean = 20 min · SD ≈ 13.5 min

Wildly unpredictable — high spread

Key insight: Both hospitals have the same mean wait time. But Hospital B's high standard deviation signals a serious process problem — some patients wait 2 minutes, others 35. The mean alone hides this.

Standard Deviation · Excel Syntax

Two versions — know which to use

=STDEV.S(number1, [number2], ...)
// Sample standard deviation — use when your data
// is a SAMPLE from a larger population (most common)

=STDEV.P(number1, [number2], ...)
// Population standard deviation — use ONLY when
// you have data for the ENTIRE population

Business rule: Unless you have data for every single observation (every patient, every sale, every employee), use STDEV.S.

Manual formula

s = √[ Σ(x − x̄)² ÷ (n−1) ]
where x̄ = sample mean, n = count

Step by step

  1. Find the mean
  2. Subtract mean from each value
  3. Square each difference
  4. Sum the squares
  5. Divide by (n−1)
  6. Take the square root

Analyzing nurse overtime hours

Mean = 8 hrsΣ(x−x̄)² = 40

// =STDEV.S(B2:B6) → 3.16

What does 3.16 tell us?

Most nurses worked within about ±3 hours of the 8-hour mean. The overtime schedule is fairly balanced — no one is dramatically overloaded.

DECISION USE

If SD jumped to 8+ hours, HR would flag a scheduling problem and investigate workload fairness.

Correlation · The Concept

Do two variables move together?

+1

Perfect Positive

As X increases, Y always increases in lock step

e.g. staff hours ↑ → patients seen ↑

0

No Correlation

Changes in X tell us nothing about Y

e.g. day of week vs. patient zip code

−1

Perfect Negative

As X increases, Y always decreases

e.g. wait time ↑ → satisfaction score ↓

=CORREL(array1, array2)
// Returns a coefficient between −1 and +1
// Strong: |r| > 0.7 · Moderate: 0.4–0.7 · Weak: < 0.4

Critical Thinking · The Most Important Rule

Correlation ≠ Causation

What correlation tells you

Two variables move together. When one goes up, the other tends to go up (or down).

r = +0.89 between ice cream sales and hospital admissions in July
≠

What correlation does NOT tell you

That one thing causes the other. Both could be caused by a third variable — heat in July causes both more ice cream purchases AND more heat-stroke admissions.

Harborside Example

We found r = −0.98 between wait time and satisfaction. We cannot conclude from this alone that reducing wait time causes higher satisfaction. Maybe low-severity cases (shorter waits) are inherently more satisfied regardless. Deeper analysis is needed before making a staffing investment decision.

⚠ Common Mistake

Using the mean without reporting the standard deviation

❌ INCOMPLETE — misleading

"Harborside's average patient satisfaction score is 7.6 out of 10."

✓ COMPLETE — actionable

"Harborside's average satisfaction score is 7.6 (SD = 2.4), meaning scores range widely — from 5.2 to 9.1. There is a high-variance problem to solve."

"Talking about the mean without the standard deviation is like describing the Titanic without mentioning the iceberg."

— Statistics instructor wisdom

Key Takeaways

What to carry out of this room

1

Mean, median, and mode each tell a different story. Choose the right one for your data — median when you have outliers, mode for categorical data, mean for symmetric distributions.

2

Standard deviation is the essential partner to the mean. Always report both. A high SD signals inconsistency; a low SD signals reliability — both are actionable business signals.

3

Correlation (CORREL) measures relationship strength from −1 to +1. Strong correlation is worth investigating — but it never proves causation. Always dig deeper before recommending action.
