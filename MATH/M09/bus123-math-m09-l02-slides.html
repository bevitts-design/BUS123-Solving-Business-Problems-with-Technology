Part 1 · Annuities

What Is an Annuity?

Definition

An annuity is a series of equal payments made at equal time intervals. Every mortgage, car loan, monthly retirement contribution, and lease payment is an annuity.

Two Types

Ordinary AnnuityPayment at end of period. type=0. Excel default. Most loans.
Annuity DuePayment at beginning of period. type=1. Lease payments.

Where pmt Lives in the TVM Functions

All Three Functions Use pmt
=PMT(rate, nper, pv, 0)=FV(rate, nper, pmt, pv)=PV(rate, nper, pmt, 0)

In L01 we set pmt=0 for lump sums. Today pmt is the variable of interest. Everything else stays the same — same sign convention, same period-matching rule.

Part 1 · Sign Convention

The pmt Sign Rule — Same Logic as pv

The Rule

Money leaving your pocket → negative
Money coming to you → positive

This is identical to the pv rule from M08 and M09-L01. The same logic applies to every cash flow in every TVM function.

Sanity check for loans: =PMT() result is negative (payment out). Total paid = ABS(pmt) × nper. Total interest = Total paid − original loan.

Loan Payments — pmt Negative
=PMT(rate, nper, +320000, 0)// pv positive = money received (loan proceeds)// Result: negative = monthly payment out
Savings Contributions — pmt Negative
=FV(rate, nper, −500, 0)// pmt negative = monthly deposit out// Result: positive = balance received
Part 2 · =PMT() Function

What Payment Is Required?

Excel Syntax
=PMT(rate, nper, pv, [fv], [type])// For loan payoff: fv=0 (balance reaches zero)// Result is negative — monthly payment out of pocket
rate

Period rate. Always divide annual rate by payments per year.

nper

Total payments. Years × payments per year.

pv

Loan amount. Enter as positive — money you received.

Part 3 · =FV() with Payments

Monthly Savings — Building a Retirement Account

Meridian Client Scenario

A client contributes $500/month to a retirement account earning 7% annual for 30 years. How much will they have?

Excel
=FV(7%/12, 30*12, −500, 0)// pmt negative = monthly deposit out// Returns: $606,438.29
What They Contributed
Total deposited: 500 × 360 = $180,000
Account value: $606,438
Growth from compounding: $426,438

The Power of Regular Investing

The client put in $180,000 over 30 years. Compounding turned it into $606,438 — 3.4× their total contributions.

Key insight: The earlier the contributions start, the more compounding periods each dollar has. Missing the first 5 years of contributions costs far more than $30,000 in deposits.

Starting with a Lump Sum
=FV(7%/12, 360, −500, −10000)// pv=−10000 AND pmt=−500// Both negative — both are outflows
Part 3 · =PV() with Payments

Valuing a Payment Stream — What Is It Worth Today?

Meridian Client Scenario

A client will receive $1,500/month for 10 years. Discount rate 5% annual. What is the stream worth today?

Excel
=PV(5%/12, 10*12, 1500, 0)// pmt positive = payments received// Returns: −$141,799// Negative = cost to acquire this stream today

DM Sanspreting the Result

The stream pays out $1,500 × 120 = $180,000 total. But in today's dollars at 5%, it is only worth $141,799.

The negative means outflow — what you would pay today to acquire this stream. If someone offers to sell the stream for $130,000, it's a good deal. At $155,000, it's overpriced.

Compare to DCF from M08: In M08 we discounted individual payments and summed them. =PV() with pmt does the same calculation in one step — but only for equal payments.

Part 3 · Payment Timing

Ordinary Annuity vs. Annuity Due

Ordinary Annuity (type=0, Default)

Payments at the end of each period. Most loans, mortgages, and savings plans. =PMT() and =PV() default to type=0 — omit the argument.

Annuity Due (type=1)

Payments at the beginning of each period. Leases, insurance premiums paid upfront. Each payment earns one extra period of interest — slightly higher FV, slightly lower required PMT.

Ordinary Annuity (default)
=PMT(6%/12, 60, 25000, 0, 0)// type=0 → payment at end// Returns: −$483.32/month
Annuity Due
=PMT(6%/12, 60, 25000, 0, 1)// type=1 → payment at beginning// Returns: −$480.91/month (lower)

For all activity problems: use type=0 (ordinary annuity) unless specified otherwise. This is the real-world default for loans.

The Trade-Off

Longer term → lower monthly payment → much more total interest. The 30-year borrower pays $294,612 more than the 10-year borrower over the life of the loan.

Total Interest Formula
Total Interest = ABS(PMT) × nper − PV// In Excel: =ABS(B7)*B6-B2

This is one of the most impactful calculations a financial advisor can show a client. A clear table like this drives real decisions.

⚠ Common Mistake

Annual Rate and Annual nper for a Monthly Payment Problem

❌ The Mistake

Using annual rate and years instead of monthly rate and months for a monthly payment calculation.

Wrong Formula
=PMT(6.5%, 30, 320000)// rate=annual, nper=years// Returns: −$27,006/year ← looks plausible// But monthly payment is $2,023
✓ Correct — Match Period to Payment Frequency

For monthly payments, use monthly rate and total months. Always ask: what is the payment frequency?

Correct Formula
=PMT(6.5%/12, 30*12, 320000)// rate=monthly nper=months// Returns: −$2,023.13/month ✓

Key Takeaways

Three Things That Last

01 — =PMT()
=PMT(rate, nper, pv, 0)

Result is negative — outflow. Read as absolute value. Total interest = ABS(pmt)×nper − pv.

02 — =FV() with pmt
=FV(rate, nper, −pmt, −pv)

Both pmt and pv negative for regular investing. Result positive — balance received.

03 — =PV() with pmt
=PV(rate, nper, +pmt, 0)

pmt positive for received payments. Result negative — cost to acquire the stream today.
