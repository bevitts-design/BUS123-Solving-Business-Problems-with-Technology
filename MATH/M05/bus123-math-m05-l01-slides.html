Scheduling Decision

One Person at 52 Hours or Two at 26?

Overtime can solve a short staffing gap.

It can also hide a permanent headcount problem.

When you see sustained overtime every week, ask: is this a scheduling choice, or a capacity decision?

Depreciation

Not Cash Leaving Today

Depreciation is an accounting estimate of the asset cost used up during a period.

The cash left when the equipment was purchased. Depreciation recognizes the expense across the years that benefit.
Book value = cost − accumulated depreciation

Asset cost

Purchase price plus ready-for-use costs.

capitalized

Residual value

Expected value at end of useful life.

not depreciated

Useful life

How long the firm expects service.

time horizon

Book value

Undepreciated cost remaining.

cost − accumulated

Straight-Line Method

Predictable. Transparent. Auditable.

Spreads the depreciable cost evenly across useful life.

Useful when an asset provides steady service — like a diagnostic unit used at the same rate every month.
Annual depreciation = (cost − residual value) ÷ useful life =(84000-12000)/6 → $12,000 per year
Book value starts high ($84K)
Falls evenly to residual ($12K)

⚠ Common Mistake

Confusing Depreciation Expense with Accumulated Depreciation

The Mix-Up

Students write "book value = cost − this year's depreciation."

That only works in Year 1. In Year 3, accumulated depreciation is 3 years of expense — not one.

Book value = cost − ACCUMULATED depreciation (not just one year's expense)
Year 3 Example
Expense (each year): $12,000
Accumulated (after 3 years): $36,000
Book value: $84,000 − $36,000 = $48,000

NOT $84,000 − $12,000 = $72,000 ✗

Key Takeaways

Make Operating Costs Visible

1
Pay type shapes incentives — match the formula to the role, not just to what is easiest to administer.
2
Overtime changes payroll quickly — a few extra hours per employee compound across a full clinical team.
3
Straight-line depreciation makes long-run asset cost predictable — and book value is cost minus accumulated, not one year.
