Passive User vs. Professional Operator

05
Passive User
Uses technology others configured
  • Opens spreadsheets someone else built
  • Types numbers into pre-made cells
  • Saves files wherever they land by default
  • Doesn't know why a formula works
  • Asks: "What does this button do?"
Professional Operator
Builds tools to solve problems
  • Designs the spreadsheet from a blank sheet
  • Writes formulas and understands every calculation
  • Maintains a file system others can navigate
  • Can explain any output to a client
  • Asks: "Which tool is right for this problem?"
🎯

BUS123 moves you from the left column to the right. By semester end, you will be someone who builds tools, not just someone who uses them.

Harborside: What Is Payer Mix?

09

Payer mix is the breakdown of where a hospital's revenue comes from — by insurance type. It drives almost every major business decision Harborside makes.

Why It Matters
  • Medicare and Medicaid reimburse at lower rates than private insurance
  • A 5% payer mix shift can mean millions in annual revenue impact
  • Staffing, supply budgets, and expansion plans all depend on it
  • Finance teams recalculate this every month — in Excel
Example: Harborside Payer Mix (Simplified)
  • Private Insurance — 45% of revenue
  • Medicare — 32% of revenue
  • Medicaid — 15% of revenue
  • Self-Pay / Uninsured — 8% of revenue
📐

Calculating payer mix percentages is one of the first real-world applications you'll build in EXCEL-M01. This is exactly where math meets business.

Speaker Notes — Slide 09
Key Point

Direct preview of MATH-M01 content. Students see the real destination — the math isn't abstract, it runs a hospital's finances.

Real-World Hook

Ask: 'If Medicare pays 70 cents on every dollar a private insurer pays, and your Medicare share jumps from 32% to 40%, what happens to revenue?' That's the CFO's daily question.

Common Misconception

Students think percentages are 'basic.' Payer mix requires interpreting what percentages mean in context — that interpretive layer makes a professional.

  • Payer mix recalculated monthly, informing every budget decision

Common Mistake

12
⚠️
Confusing "I've Used It" With "I Know It"

Most students have used Excel, Word, and PowerPoint before. Very few have used them professionally — producing output someone else depends on.

  • "I've typed things into Excel" ≠ building a payroll workbook for six employees with different pay structures that processes correctly every week.
  • "I've made a slide deck" ≠ producing a presentation a CFO will show to a hospital board.
  • "I know how to save files" ≠ maintaining a consistent, navigable file system a team can use without your help.
  • Be honest about your starting point. The gap between casual familiarity and professional competence is exactly what this course closes.

Where AI Fits In — Honestly

14

AI tools — including Microsoft Copilot built into Excel — are part of real business workflows today. Here's a clear-eyed take:

AI Is Good At
  • Suggesting formulas for problems you describe clearly
  • Drafting first versions of documents and slide text
  • Summarizing large datasets in plain language
  • Spotting patterns in data you provide
AI Is Not Good At
  • Knowing your organization's specific context and rules
  • Catching errors in data it wasn't told to verify
  • Making judgment calls about what a number means
  • Building professional credibility — you have to do that
⚖️

People who use AI most effectively understand the underlying work well enough to verify what AI produces. That's the professional operator — and what BUS123 builds.

Speaker Notes — Slide 14
Key Point

AI is not the replacement for this course — it's the reason this course matters more.

Real-World Hook

Harborside: if Copilot suggests the wrong denominator in a payer mix formula and you can't spot it, you've given the CFO bad data. Real consequence.

Common Misconception

Some students think AI means they don't need to learn Excel. Without Excel fluency, you can't quality-check what AI generates. You become a liability.

Timing

~4 min. Expect pushback. Acknowledge the debate, then redirect: 'That's why we learn this the right way.'

Technology Habits That Pay Off

16
  • Name files so others can find them — not just you, right now. A well-named file signals organizational competence.
  • Save as you work — OneDrive autosave helps, but the habit matters more than the feature.
  • Never put critical data in one place only — know where your files physically live, not just which app you used to open them.
  • Document your formulas — a comment cell costs 30 seconds and saves hours later.
  • Back up before you edit — copy a working file before major changes. Version history helps; the habit protects.
  • Verify before you present — open the file on a different device first. What renders on your laptop may break on the conference room screen.
  • Use the desktop app — browser Office is limited. Anything you submit should be built in the full app.
  • Check permissions before sharing — verify who can open a OneDrive file. Privacy is a professional responsibility.
Speaker Notes — Slide 16
Key Point

Habits are the invisible professional differentiator. Identical technical skills, very different reputations.

Real-World Hook

In healthcare, data integrity is a compliance issue. Accidentally sharing payer analysis with the wrong person creates a legal problem.

Common Misconception

Ask: 'Who has had a file named final_FINAL_v3_USE THIS ONE.xlsx?' The whole room laughs. Then: 'That's what we're fixing.'

Timing

~3 min. Keep it light — frame as insider tips, not scolding.
