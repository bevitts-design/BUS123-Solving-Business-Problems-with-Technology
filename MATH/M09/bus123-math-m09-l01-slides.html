Part 1 · Building on M08

From Simple to Compound — One Change, Exponential Impact

M08 — Simple Interest (Linear)

Interest calculated on the original principal only. Same dollar amount every period.

FV = P × (1 + R × T)
Flat growth — same $ added every year

M09 — Compound Interest (Exponential)

Interest calculated on the accumulated balance. The base grows every period.

FV = PV × (1 + i)ⁿ
// Exponential growth — more every year

The key change: simple uses R×T (multiplication). Compound uses (1+i)ⁿ (exponentiation). The exponent n is what makes the snowball roll.

Part 1 · The Snowball Effect

Year 1 — No Difference

Both give $1,050 in year 1. Compound diverges from year 2 onward, when interest begins earning on prior interest.

$4,321 vs. $2,500. Same rate. Same principal. Compound produces 73% more.

This is the engine behind every retirement account and long-term investment. The math is simple — the exponent does all the work.

Part 2 · Future Value

FV = PV × (1 + i)ⁿ

i · Rate per Period

Annual rate ÷ compounding periods per year. Must match nper's unit.

n · Number of Periods

Years × compounding periods per year. Must match i's unit.

Critical rule: i and n must always refer to the same unit of time. Mismatching is the most common compound interest error.

Part 2 · Excel =FV() Function

The =FV() Function — Syntax First

Excel Syntax
=FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv], [type])
ArgumentMeaningFor Lump Sum
rateRate per compounding periodAnnual rate ÷ periods/yr
nperTotal compounding periodsYears × periods/yr
pmtRecurring payment (annuities)Enter 0
pvPresent value (starting amount)Enter as negative
ResultFuture value at end of nperReturns positive
Lump Sum Example
=FV(6%, 5, 0, −25000)// Returns: +$33,455.64// pv negative = money out today

pmt = annuity argument. Always 0 for single lump-sum problems. We use it in M09-L02.

Part 2 · Sign Convention

Why pv Must Be Negative

Excel's Cash Flow Rule

Outflow (money leaving you): negative
Inflow (money coming to you): positive

Investing $25,000 today is an outflow — money leaves your pocket. PV = −$25,000. The FV result returns positive.

Sanity check: FV should always be larger than PV in absolute terms. If it isn't, the sign is wrong.

✓ Correct
=FV(6%, 5, 0, −25000) → +$33,456// pv negative → FV positive
✗ Wrong Sign — Still Returns a Number
=FV(6%, 5, 0, 25000) → −$33,456// No error — just wrong meaning

Part 3 · Compounding Frequency

Frequency Matters

Same principal. Same annual rate. Same years. Different ending balances. When compounding happens more often, interest starts earning interest sooner.

The Rule: Always Adjust Both

rate = annual rate ÷ periods/year
nper = years × periods/year

Never Adjust Only One

Changing nper but not rate produces a completely wrong result. Both must change together.

Part 3 · Non-Annual Compounding

$25,000 at 6% for 5 Years — What Frequency Does

Excel — Monthly Compounding
=FV(6%/12, 5*12, 0, −25000)// rate: 6%/12 nper: 5*12// Returns: $33,725.46

The Takeaway

More frequent = higher FV. Annual-to-daily gap is $290 on $25,000 over 5 years. At higher rates or longer horizons the gap grows substantially.

Part 3 · Mental Math Shortcut

The Rule of 72 — Doubling Time Estimate

Rule of 72
Years to Double ≈ 72 ÷ Rate (%)// Quick estimate — not exact// Verify precise answer with =FV()
4%
72÷4 = 18 yrs
6%
72÷6 = 12 yrs
8%
72÷8 = 9 yrs
12%
72÷12 = 6 yrs

When to Use It

Quick client conversations. Back-of-napkin planning. Sanity-checking whether an Excel answer is in the right ballpark. Does not replace exact calculation.

Try It Now

A Meridian client has a retirement account earning 7% annually. How many years to double?

72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3 years.
Verify: =FV(7%,10,0,−1000) = $1,967 ≈ $2,000 ✓

⚠ Common Mistake

Adjusting nper but Forgetting to Adjust rate

❌ The Mistake

Switching to quarterly, student updates nper to 20 but leaves rate at 6% instead of changing it to 1.5%.

Wrong Formula
=FV(6%, 20, 0, −25000)// rate=annual, nper=quarterly// Returns: $80,178 ← wildly inflated
✓ Correct — Adjust Both

Rate and nper must always refer to the same unit of time.

Correct Formula
=FV(6%/4, 5*4, 0, −25000)// rate=quarterly, nper=quarters// Returns: $33,672.54 ✓

Key Takeaways

Three Things That Last

01 — The Exponent
FV = PV × (1 + i)ⁿ

Interest earns interest. Same rate, exponentially more money the longer time passes.

02 — The Function
=FV(rate, nper, 0, −pv)

pv is negative. pmt is zero for lump sums. rate and nper must match the compounding period.

03 — Rule of 72
72 ÷ rate ≈ years to double
