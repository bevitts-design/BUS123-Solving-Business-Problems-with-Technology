Equation
The Foundation

The Fundamental Accounting Equation

Assets
What you OWN
=
Liabilities
What you OWE
+
Owner's Equity
Owner's Stake

Think of this as a perfectly balanced scale. Every single transaction in the history of a business must keep this equation in balance — this is the "double-entry" principle.

Why it matters: If a hospital buys a new MRI machine, it either pays cash (assets trade) or takes out a loan (assets up, liabilities up). The equation always stays balanced.

Balance Sheet
Statement #1

The Balance Sheet — A Financial Snapshot

🏥

Assets

Current: Cash, Accounts Receivable (patient billing), Medical Supplies

Non-Current: Buildings, MRI machines, equipment

Depreciation spreads the cost of long-term equipment over its useful life

💳

Liabilities

Accounts Payable — owe to medical suppliers
Salaries Payable — staff wages earned but unpaid
Long-term Loans — bank financing for equipment
Taxes Payable

📊

Owner's Equity

The residual — what's left for owners after all debts are paid.

Assets − Liabilities = Equity

Grows through Retained Earnings (profits kept in the business)

The Income Statement — Did We Make Money?

=Net Sales − COGS − Operating Expenses Net Sales = Gross Sales − Returns − Discounts
COGS = Beg. Inventory + Net Purchases − End. Inventory
Net Income = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses

For a service business like Harborside, COGS is often replaced by "Cost of Services." For retail, COGS is the direct product cost.

Cash vs Profit
The Critical Distinction

"Cash is King" — Here's Why

📄 Income Statement

Accrual basis: Revenue recorded when earned, expenses when incurred — regardless of when cash moves.

Answers: Did we profit?

💰 Cash Flow Statement

Cash basis: Only records when actual dollars move in or out of the bank account.

Answers: Can we pay our bills?

⚠️ The Gap

Harborside billed $680K in patient services. Until insurance pays, that's on the books as profit — but there's no cash yet. Payroll still has to be met.

Profit ≠ Cash
Poor cash flow management is the #1 reason small businesses fail — even profitable ones.

Trend Analysis
Analysis Tool #1

Trend Analysis — Percentage of Base Year

=Current Year Amount / Base Year Amount → Result expressed as a %
Base year = 100% always
Two Steps:
1. Designate the base year (= 100%)
2. Divide each year by the base year amount — round to nearest whole %
2024 (Base)20252026
Net Revenue$3,800,000$4,100,000$4,250,000
Trend %100%108%112%
Net Income$320,000$390,000$450,000
Trend %100%122%141%
Revenue grew 12% — but net income grew 41%. Harborside is getting more efficient.

Financial Ratio Analysis

Ratio Excel Formula What It Tells You
Current Ratio =B4/B5

⚠ Mistake
⚠ Common Mistake

Confusing Profit with Cash

❌ The Mistake

Harborside had $450K in net income, so a new manager assumes there's plenty of cash to buy equipment without a loan. They look at the income statement and feel confident.

✓ The Reality

$680K is still in Accounts Receivable — the insurance companies haven't paid yet. Actual cash on hand is only $412K. Always check the cash flow statement, not just the income statement.

Rule: Net Income is an accounting number. Cash in the bank is the survival number.

Takeaways
What to Remember

3 Things That Will Stick

1
The Scale Never Tips

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Always. If your balance sheet doesn't balance, you're missing a transaction.

2
Profit and Cash Are Different Animals

A business can show net income and still run out of cash. Always check the cash flow statement before making financial decisions.

3
Ratios Only Mean Something in Context

A 10% profit margin is excellent in healthcare but weak in software. Always compare to industry averages and prior years.
